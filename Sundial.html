A photo

The word "photography" was created from the Greek roots φωτός (phōtos), genitive of φῶς (phōs), "light" and γραφή (graphé) "representation by means of lines" or "drawing", together meaning "drawing with light".

Typically, a lens is used to focus the light reflected or emitted from objects into a real image on the light-sensitive surface inside a camera during a timed exposure. With an electronic image sensor, this produces an electrical charge at each pixel, which is electronically processed and stored in a digital image file for subsequent display or processing. It is a work of art to play with light either it is sunlight or artificial light.

This process of documenting light is highly related to the sun, the emision core to send light on earth. Our eyes capture things by receiving the reflected information from them. And the shadow is the dark space that light can't reach out to. Our ancestors descover the relationship between the sun and the shadow which can indicate time. They invented a simple method by documenting the shadow change drop on the platform overtime to track a day. Anthough the time accuracy is far lower than our method today, this interaction builds up a simple and beautiful bridge between us and the nature.



Other than today's digital photography, the precursor method for people to document our surrondings is slow and rough. The first group of photographers are actually painters and technologists. Before the chemical processing method was invented, painters would put their canvas on the window of the camera obscura to draw an accurate image that can best illustrate the correct vanishing points and depict a very realistic work. That was the days when realistic paints were on the top of billboard. When painters used this method, they were using their brushes to documenting the sunlight that reflected by objects.
